Finding
Compact = less surface = less loss
A cube (X1≈1.0) has minimum surface area for its volume. Every notch or recess adds surface and more heat exchange.
RF: 50/100 — second only to glazing.
Anomaly
Building 5 breaks the pattern
B5 (Long bar, X1=0.79) records the highest HL of all 7m buildings at 38.6 kWh/m² — despite higher compactness than B6.
Elongated form maximises exposure.
Design implication
Irregular forms carry a cost
L, T, Z and U forms all have lower compactness. Every articulation of the envelope adds surface area.
Every 0.1 drop in compactness adds ~4–6 kWh/m².

Key observation
X6 (orientation) has zero predictive power — N/E/S/W buildings differ by less than 0.12 kWh/m². Early-stage design can safely deprioritize orientation when glazing and form are undecided.
Glazing alone explains more variance than all other 7 parameters combined.
How Random Forest works
The ensemble method used to rank parameter importance
The algorithm — step by step
1
Bootstrap sampling
Each of the 500 decision trees is trained on a random 63% subset of the 768 simulations (sampling with replacement). The remaining 37% forms the out-of-bag (OOB) validation set.
2
Random feature selection
At each split, only √8 ≈ 3 features are considered. This decorrelates trees and prevents one dominant variable (glazing) from appearing in every split — forcing the model to explore all 8 parameters.
3
Ensemble prediction
Final prediction = average of all 500 trees. Averaging cancels individual tree errors and dramatically reduces variance compared to a single deep decision tree.
4
Importance scoring
Each feature's importance = average reduction in node impurity (mean squared error) when that feature is used as a split, across all trees. Scores are normalized to 0–100. Glazing (X7) scores 93; orientation (X6) scores 0.
RF vs IRLS — why it matters
Random Forest
Captures non-linear interactions between variables. A building that is simultaneously compact and glazed behaves differently than the sum of its parts. RF models this interaction directly.

0.51

IRLS — Iteratively Reweighted Least Squares
Linear regression variant. Assumes each variable contributes independently and additively. Fails to capture interaction effects — a fundamental limitation for architectural energy modelling.

Validation method
10-fold cross-validation repeated 100 times. The dataset is split into 10 equal folds; 9 folds train the model, 1 fold tests it. This repeats 10 times (each fold as test once), then the entire process runs 100 times with different random splits. Final MAE = mean of 1,000 test evaluations.
10 folds × 100 repetitions = 1,000 evaluations
Ecotect simulation methodology
How the 768 energy values were generated
Simulation pipeline
Geometry definition
12 building forms modelled as orthogonal volumes. Each form maintains a constant net floor area of 771.75 m² at two heights — 3.5m and 7m — yielding 24 base geometries. Form variations include Cubic, Rectangle, L-form, Plus, Long bar, U-form, Wide rect., T-form.
Glazing parametrisation
Four glazing area ratios applied to each geometry: 0%, 10%, 25%, 40% of total floor area. Five glazing distribution patterns control which facade faces receive the glass (uniform, north-heavy, south-heavy, east-heavy, west-heavy).
Thermal simulation
Ecotect performs an annual thermal simulation for each configuration using the admittance method. Heating and cooling loads are calculated as the energy required to maintain 19°C (heating) and 26°C (cooling) set-points throughout the year.
Output normalisation
Results reported as kWh/m² (per unit floor area), enabling direct comparison across building forms of different surface-to-volume ratios. Four orientations (N/E/S/W) are applied to each configuration, completing the 768-row dataset.

Scope & limitations
The dataset isolates geometric parameters only. Material properties, HVAC systems, occupancy patterns, and climate variability are held constant. This makes it ideal for studying form–energy relationships but means results should not be taken as absolute energy predictions for real buildings.
Results are relative: use them to compare forms, not to estimate absolute consumption.
