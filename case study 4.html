01 — Deciding What to Show

The Story

The main message is that Wright's work was not equally spread across America. A large number of his buildings were concentrated in a small number of cities, especially in Illinois. We wanted to show this concentration visually, while also making it possible to see every single building by name.

A radial tree was the right choice because it shows three levels at the same time: state, city, and building name. The viewer can see the big picture and the detail together.

02 — Defining the Audience

Audience

The intended audience is architecture students, academics, and people who are generally interested in architectural history. This audience is familiar with Wright's work and would find value in seeing the geographic spread of his residential projects in detail.

03 — Designing the Visualization

Design Decisions

The visualization was built using D3.js in Observable, then exported as an HTML file. The radial tidy tree layout was chosen directly from D3's library and adapted for this dataset.

Dark Background

Creates a focused atmosphere that matches the historical and archival nature of the dataset.

Three Color Levels

Amber for the root (Wright), orange for states, yellow for cities, and brown for buildings to ensure each level is readable.

Hover Tooltip

When the user moves over a state or city node, a tooltip shows the name and number of buildings. This adds an interactive, exploratory layer.

04 — Evaluation & Reflection

What the Data Reveals

The visualization confirms the expected finding: Illinois, Wisconsin, and Michigan are the most represented states. However, an unexpected finding emerged: within Illinois, Oak Park and River Forest have far more buildings than Chicago itself, suggesting Wright's practice was rooted in these suburbs, not the city center. This level of detail would not be visible in a simple bar chart.
